Parts of a Mushroom

Parts of a mushroom

Generally speaking, there are 7 parts to a mushroom.

  • Cap
  • Gills
  • Ring
  • Stem
  • Volva
  • Mycelium
  • Spore

Cap

This part is pretty straightforward; A cap of a mushroom is essentially it’s cap. The cap usually resembles a dome and acts like an umbrella to protect the gills. It comes in a variety of colours and patterns.

Gills

If a mushroom were an apple, the gills would be the apple core. This is the section of mushroom which houses and produces the spores.

Gills of a mushroom

Ring

The ring is the leftover membrane which surrounds the stem after the spores are released. Originally, they would have covered the gills from underneath. However, as the gills develop, the ring is pushed outwards, eventually left to linger around the stem. Well, at least it looks somewhat fashonable.

Stem

The stem is(obviously) the stem of the mushroom. It is a cylindrical structure that holds up the cap, and connects it to the mycelium. In some mushroom species, the stem is hollow.

Volva

Similar to the ring, the volva is the remains of a veil which would have covered the mushroom. In this case, the membrane formerly protected the mushroom in its premature stages. As the mushroom sprouts, it tears through the volva, which is then left circling the bottom of the stem.

Mushroom emerging from volva

Mycelium

A mushroom’s mycelium are basically it’s roots. This gathers nutrients from the surface which the mushroom is stationed on, usually decaying trees. The mycelium is located underground, and can expand to form a vast network.

Spore

Spores are the seeds of mushrooms, and are nested inside the gills until their release. A single mushroom can contain millions of spores. Since spores are microscopic, they can be carried by air, which they use to travel.







Classification

Mushrooms can be divided into 3 groups, depending on their method of extracting nutrients.

Saprophytes mushroom: Ink cap

Saprophytes

Saprophytes are organisms which feed off dead and decaying organic matter, such as logs and leaves. In mushrooms, this includes many species which are used in medicine and cooking, such as white button, crimini, shiitake and oyster mushrooms.

Parasite mushroom: Ophiocordyceps unilateralis

Parasites

Parasites are organisms which steal nutrients from other living organisims, without giving anything to their victim in return. These victims are called hosts. In the case of mushrooms, hosts are often arthropods. One infamous parasitic mushroom known for it’s brutal attacks on insects is Ophiocordyceps unilateralis.

Mycorrhiza mushroom: Truffle

Mycorrhiza

Mycorrhiza are creatures which form a mutualistic symbiotic relationship with their host. This means that though they take nutrients from their partner, they also provide other nutrients or benifits in return. These conditions are hard to maintain and harvest, so mycorrrhiza mushrooms are only found in nature.
